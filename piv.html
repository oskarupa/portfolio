Competence Development

A summary of my development of Industrial Design

PAST

How learning activities in Y1 and Y2 affected my Professional Identity as a designer

In the first two years of Industrial design, my development was leading me to finding a specific area within the broad field of ID. Year 1 was about exploring general competencies, while Year 2 was about personalizing my development. I immediately focused on my technical foundations in game design and built my professional identity around that.

I chose a squad and electives that developed my technical skills with Unity. I became a confident prototype and built my PI on viewing the world through the lens of video games, believing that core principles of good game design like feedback and exploration can apply to all interactive products.

This period was crucial to mastering both hard skills and soft skills. I gained experience with technical tools, but also worked in many different group dynamics, worked with a client (DAF) and understood the importance of planning my study program to develop myself.

PRESENT

While previous years were mostly about acquiring skills, my B3.1 was about testing them in contrasting environments. I balanced a mostly technical individual project with collaborative electives. Overall, while it was helpful at highlighting the differences in how I work when building technology vs. designing meaningful products. The goal of my FBP will be to combine both approaches into one project.

WORKING INDIVIDUALLY

With my B31 Squad project, I learnt the vulnerabilities of working alone. Without teammates I gained full individual freedom to plan my focus and work on ideas that interest me. However, I also learnt how I can fall into an engineering mindset. Getting too focused on the functionality of my prototype resulted in me only working towards getting it done instead of making smaller iterations, gathering feedback, and making changes. It also limited my development of other experience areas by focusing only on what I am good at.

TECHNOLOGY & REALIZATION

B31 Squad Project (Gen Abstract): I learnt to use a new program that I did not have prior experience with, Touch Designer. I proved my ability to self-tech complex programs. I built a fully functional physical interface that communicated with the computer in real time. Overall, this project shows my expertise in technology and realization, but what it lacked was exploring the other expertise areas through these prototypes.

MATH, DATA & COMPUTING

Digital Craftsmanship: In this elective, I understood the high potential of the combination of two expertise areas: MDC and CA. We worked on a project where we gathered data on our physical movement patterns, specifically walking outside. We used programming explorations to create nature-inspired digital visualizations of the data and explored how to physically materialize them into a designed bag. We used the generated patterns as a template to hand sew them using different fabrics. This elective is a clear example of using one expertise area I’m good at to further develop in another (MDC -> CA), a practice that I plan on experiencing in my FBP.

CREATIVITY & AESTHETICS

I developed mostly in creativity and aesthetics through the connection to other expertise areas. I’m beginning to understand the value of this expertise area. In Gen Abstract, aesthetics was defined by interaction, sliders, knobs and how the program responded visually in real time. In Digital Craftsmanship, aesthetics was built around creatively visualizing data we gathered. By using a generative visualization, we not only communicate the data, but also our inspiration of patterns found in nature.

BUSINESS & ENTREPRENEURSHIP

New Product Marketing: I took this course to understand products from a slightly different perspective. In marketing we learnt a lot of things connected to understanding the user and market. Similarly, to DIM, a lot of the course was based on learning frameworks and tools. Overall, I still struggle to find connections between Business & Entrepreneurship to other Expertise areas.

Introduction to Futures thinking offered a perspective on design from an industry-over-academic perspective. It was powerful to understand the different methods groups of designers due to create value for their clients. Most powerful was the final presentation of people who have important roles in big organizations who came from a similar background to where we are now. It showed the potential scale of change that we as designers can make on the world.

DESIGN & RESEARCH

Introduction to Futures thinking was structured around workshops, where we often worked with overwhelming quantities of data. For example, everyone was tasked with bringing photos of appliances from stores and online. That meant we had hundreds of different printed out appliances to analyze and sort.

Here, I learnt the power of first impressions and intuition when designing. Often through different academic learning activities (for example the marketing course), I thought professional design decisions should be based on studies, user tests, and data. In futures thinking a different technique was explored, using our designer intuition. When going through hundreds of appliances, we sorted them based on feelings. When mapping the perception of brands by consumers, we used our own opinions. Overall, it was a powerful realization that difficult design decisions can be made through these methods.
